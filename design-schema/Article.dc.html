Safety Form Usability: The Friction Budget Method

Every required field beyond what a worker can complete honestly turns your safety database into compliance fiction. The Friction Budget puts a number on it.

Serhat Demirkol
EHS Product · Systems · Data

FIG 1 — Every field is a tax on the truth of the record.

Ask any frontline crew what happens at the end of a shift when the app demands fourteen fields to log a near-miss, and you will hear the same answer in every language: they put in whatever makes the screen go away. The data is clean. The data is complete. The data is fiction.

This is not a training problem, and it is not a discipline problem. It is an interface economics problem. Every required field, every mandatory dropdown, every "please describe in detail" box spends something — the worker's time, attention, and willingness to tell you the truth. Spend more than they have, and they don't stop submitting. They stop being honest.

I call the amount they have to spend the friction budget. It is the total cognitive and temporal cost a worker will absorb before the marginal field stops producing real information and starts producing plausible noise. The budget is small, it is measurable, and almost every EHS form on the market blows through it by design.

01

What a field actually costs

A form field looks free. It is a line in a schema, a column in a table, a checkbox in a requirements doc that someone ticked because it seemed useful to have. But the cost of a field is not paid by the person who specced it. It is paid, in full, by the person standing in the rain at 6pm holding a phone with cold hands.

Break the cost into three parts. There is the read cost — the effort to parse what the field is asking. There is the recall cost — the effort to retrieve or reconstruct the answer. And there is the decision cost — the effort to choose between options that often overlap or don't fit the situation at all. The third is the silent killer: a dropdown of twenty injury types where none of them is quite right doesn't collect data, it manufactures it.

Workers don't abandon forms that are long. They abandon forms that are dishonest about how long they are.

This is why progress bars matter more than field count, and why a form that shows its whole shape up front outperforms one that reveals fields conditionally. Hidden length is a betrayal of the budget — the worker commits, then discovers the cost was higher than quoted, and recalibrates by lowering the quality of every remaining answer.

02

Budgeting the form

The method is deliberately crude, because crude is what gets used. Assign each field a friction score, sum the form, and compare it against the budget for the context in which the form is filled out. A desk-based incident review has a large budget. A one-handed entry on a scaffold has almost none.

The friction score
Pre-filled / sensor-captured0
Single tap (yes/no, scan)1
Bounded choice (≤5 options)2
Open choice (long list / search)4
Free text requiring judgement6
Field budget for one-handed mobile entry: ~8. Most incident forms score north of 30.

The numbers are not the point — the ordering is. The moment you score a form this way, the conversation in the room changes. It stops being "what would we like to know" and becomes "what can we afford to ask." Those are different forms, and only one of them gets filled out truthfully.

Spend the budget where it earns signal. A sensor that captures location and timestamp for free buys back the budget you need for the one free-text field that actually matters: what happened, in your words. That field is worth six on its own. Protect it by making everything around it cost nothing.
